SAINT SEIYA

"PEGASUS COMET FIST!!!!"

In ancient times..... 


With Saint Seiya: Knights of the Zodiac, Netflix no doubt hopes to get more American eyes on a Japanese phenomenon. The Saint Seiya property has been massively popular in its native country since the 1980s, thanks to a manga series that sold in the tens of millions, as well as an influential animated TV program, spinoff films, toys and other typical capitalist-franchise whatnot. Of course, this is sacred ground for anime fans, who are likely to bray their dismay upon seeing their beloved stories reiterated in 21st-century CG animation and infiltrated with smartphones. But will it appeal to a new audience?

DEEP BREATH: There’s a dark prophecy surrounding the latest rebirth of Athena. Five warriors are sworn to protect her. She’s a baby with purple hair. (I’m simplifying this significantly for brevity, and possibly for the sake of our sanity.) Cut to the home of a young boy and his sister. It’s night. Patricia’s hands are glowing blue. Black-clad, heavily armed private-security-looking goons blast the crap outta their house. A golden knight materializes out of nowhere to protect them. He deflects a barrage of bullets with his mighty goldness. Patricia’s injured, knocked unconscious. Seiya (voice of Crocket Groves) stands by as the knight picks her up and zaps off into the night like a gilded comet.

An undisclosed amount of time later, Seiya is a teenage skateboarder (Bryson Baugus) who carries a crayon drawing of his lost sister in his pocket. In the middle of a pummeling by bullies, his hands glow blue, and he levitates a car off the ground. This being the Hashtag Era, the bullies caught it on video, and it goes viral. Everyone at the orphanage knows Seiya is, like, a total freak.

But. Does destiny call? Of course it does! Seiya is drugged and kidnapped by benevolent forces who want to show him how powerful he is, and what his destiny is, and possibly where his sister is — even though benevolent forces probably shouldn’t be drugging and kidnapping people? As these things inevitably go, he’s taken to a hidden lair inside a mountain. He wakes up and meets Alman Kido (Marty Fleck), an oldish graybeard, and his purple-haired granddaughter Sienna (Emily Neves), who sure looks like she might be Athena reborn? We’ll see. Kido spends the next several minutes of screentime explaining over cutaway flashbacks how there are powerful gods at war with his former business partner Vander Guraad (James Belcher), who wants to steal their power, and a group of zodiac-themed knights wants to prevent the war from destroying the world, and Seiya is one of those knights, a bronze one called Pegasus, and somehow, his lost sister is tied up in all this.

Before Seiya can say Kido’s story sounds like so much flying horse crap, Guraad shows up with his insanely spiky hair, scarred-up face, general’s uniform, one mottled eye and his private-security goons, engages in a philosophical argument with Kido, and threatens to murder Sienna. I assume he’s the bad guy. The goons take Seiya and Sienna. Seiya’s hands glow blue, and he blasts one of Guraad’s helicopters out of the air with a fistful of laser bolts. Guraad vows to capture the Pegasus.

Without the weight of decades of franchise history, this version of Saint Seiya is reasonably compelling, a familiar chosen-one-type story not too far removed from classical comic-book tropes. To my eye, Seiya has a Peter Parker-ish character arc ahead of him — he’s a gifted kid who’s experienced some tragedy and learns lessons about responsibility. It also features a totally literal leap-of-faith moment for Seiya, when he learns he can jump out of an airplane without a parachute and survive.
